Kenneth C. Wong

Research

My research sits at the intersection of cosmology and extragalactic astronomy. I primarily use gravitational lensing — the bending of light by massive structures — as a probe of galaxy evolution, dark matter, and the large-scale properties of the Universe. Below is an overview of my main research areas.

Research Areas

[figure: strong lensing arc]

Strong Gravitational Lensing

When a massive foreground galaxy or cluster nearly perfectly aligns with a background source, the result is dramatic arcs or Einstein rings. I use these systems to constrain mass distributions and measure cosmological distances.

[figure: galaxy cluster / dark matter map]

Dark Matter in Galaxies & Clusters

Lensing provides a direct, model-independent window into the distribution of dark matter. My work examines how dark matter halos are structured and how their properties depend on environment and cosmic time.

[figure: H0 tension diagram]

Measuring the Hubble Constant

Time-delay cosmography with strongly lensed quasars offers a competitive, independent route to H0. I am a member of the H0LiCOW/TDCOSMO collaboration, working to reduce systematic uncertainties in this measurement.

[figure: galaxy SED / photometry]

Galaxy Evolution

Lensing magnification lets us study faint, high-redshift galaxies in otherwise-inaccessible detail. I combine photometric and spectroscopic data to track how galaxy structure and stellar populations change over cosmic time.
